### Hook — Start with the LSTM's breaking point

Say this out loud, naturally:

> *"So — we just spent a whole part on LSTMs. And they're great. They can handle
> sequences, they have memory, they can do sentiment analysis and named entity
> recognition. But I want to show you where they break.*
>
> *Imagine you're translating this sentence from English to French:*
> 'The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired.'
>
> *What does 'it' refer to? The animal, right? Not the street.*
> *You figured that out immediately. How?*
>
> *You looked at the WHOLE sentence at once. You jumped back to 'animal.'
> You didn't have to process every word in order to get there.*
>
> *An LSTM can't do that. It processes left to right, squeezes everything
> into one hidden vector, and by the time it gets to 'tired', information
> about 'animal' may have decayed beyond recovery.*
>
> *That's called the bottleneck problem. And in 2017, a team at Google
> wrote a paper called 'Attention Is All You Need' that changed everything.*"

Write on the board:

```
LSTM bottleneck:

"The" → h1 → "cat" → h2 → "sat" → h3 → "on" → h4 → CONTEXT VECTOR c
                                                              ↓
                                                         Decoder
                                              (must generate from only c)

For long sentences, c forgets early words.
```

> *"The fix was elegant: instead of compressing everything into one vector,
> let the decoder directly look back at every single encoder state.
> But not equally — it CHOOSES which ones to focus on.
> That's attention."*

## SECTION 1: The RNN Bottleneck  (15 min)

### Explain, then draw

> *"Before attention, the seq2seq model worked like this:*
> *Encoder reads the whole input, step by step, left to right.*
> *At the end, you get one context vector c.*
> *The decoder then generates the output — from only that one vector.*
>
> *Here's the problem: what if your input is 100 words long?*
> *That one vector has to capture every word, every relationship, every nuance.*
> *It's like trying to summarize an entire book in one sentence.*
> *Some information always gets lost."*

**Draw this on the whiteboard:**

```
ENCODER (reads input L→R):
┌─────┐   ┌─────┐   ┌─────┐   ┌─────┐   ┌─────┐
│ h1  │→  │ h2  │→  │ h3  │→  │ h4  │→  │ h5  │ = c
└─────┘   └─────┘   └─────┘   └─────┘   └─────┘
"The"     "old"     "man"    "walked"   "slowly"

DECODER (generates output from only c):
c → "Le" → "vieux" → "homme" → "marchait" → "lentement"

Problem: by step 5 of the decoder, how much does c
still "remember" about "The"?
```

**Ask the room:**
> *"In your own intuition — does this seem like a good design?
> What would YOU do differently if you had to design this?"*

Let them think. Good answers:
- "Let the decoder look at all the hidden states?" — YES, that's exactly it
- "Use a bigger vector?" — valid but doesn't scale, leads to another question
- "Process it backwards?" — good instinct, bidirectional RNNs exist but still have the vector problem

> *"Exactly. The solution is: give the decoder access to ALL encoder states.
> But not equally — let it weight them. Focus on the relevant ones.
> That's the attention mechanism, proposed by Bahdanau in 2014."*

## SECTION 2: Query, Key, Value — The Spotlight Analogy  (20 min)

### The main conceptual model — use this analogy every time

> *"Here's the mental model I want you to carry for the rest of Part 6.*
> *Think of a theater stage.*
>
> *There are several actors on stage — those are the Keys and Values.*
> *The Query is a spotlight operator.*
>
> *The spotlight operator says: 'I'm looking for the actor who plays the villain.'*
> *Some actors match that description better than others.*
> *The spotlight shines brightest on the best match.*
> *Then we take what those actors say — weighted by how bright the spotlight is.*
> *That weighted combination is the attention output."*

**Draw Q/K/V on the board — draw it every time you explain attention:**

```
       ATTENTION = "soft database lookup"

Query (Q):  "What am I looking for?"
            (from the decoder's current state — what it needs to generate next)

Key   (K):  "What does each position contain?"
            (from each encoder hidden state — a label/description)

Value (V):  "What is the actual content?"
            (from each encoder hidden state — the actual information)

Step 1: Compare Q with every K → get a SCORE for each position
Step 2: softmax(scores) → get WEIGHTS that sum to 1
Step 3: weighted sum of Values = ATTENTION OUTPUT

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q × K^T / sqrt(d_k)) × V
```

> *"The score tells us: how relevant is this Key to our Query?*
> *High score = this position is relevant = gets a high weight.*
> *Low score = irrelevant = gets close to zero weight.*
>
> *The softmax turns those scores into probabilities.*
> *Then we take a weighted average of all the Values.*
> *So instead of picking ONE answer, we get a smooth mix.*
> *That's why it's called SOFT attention — no hard decisions, just weights."*

**Interactive moment — ask the room:**

> *"Give me a sentence. Any sentence."*

Take whatever they give. Say it's "The bank can guarantee deposits."

> *"Now — when translating 'bank' to French, there are two French words:*
> *'banque' (financial) and 'rive' (riverbank).*
> *The decoder needs to figure out which one.*
> *Its Query is like asking: 'is this a financial context?'*
> *It looks at the Keys for 'guarantee', 'deposits' — those score HIGH.*
> *It looks at 'river', 'water' — those are absent, score zero.*
> *The weighted output tells it: financial context. Use 'banque'.*
> *That's attention solving a real disambiguation problem."*

### The scaling factor — why divide by sqrt(d_k)?

> *"One subtle but important detail — why do we divide by sqrt(d_k)?*
>
> *When vectors are large — say, dimension 512 — the dot products*
> *Q·K can get very large numbers.*
> *When you put large numbers into softmax, you get values very close to 0 or 1.*
> *That means almost all the weight goes to one position.*
> *The gradient of softmax in that region is nearly zero — the model stops learning.*
>
> *Dividing by sqrt(d_k) keeps the dot products in a reasonable range.*
> *It's a normalization trick that stabilizes training."*

**Write on board:**

```
WITHOUT scaling:    Q·K = [100, 2, -50]  →  softmax ≈ [1.0, 0.0, 0.0]  BAD
WITH scaling / √64: Q·K = [12.5, 0.25, -6.25] → softmax ≈ [0.9, 0.1, 0.0]  BETTER
```

## SECTION 4: Self-Attention vs Cross-Attention  (15 min)

> *"Now — a quick but important distinction.*
>
> *Everything we've seen is CROSS-attention: Q comes from the decoder,
> K and V come from the encoder. The decoder looks at the encoder.*
>
> *But Transformers also use SELF-attention: Q, K, and V ALL come from
> the same sequence. Each position looks at every other position in the SAME sequence.*
>
> *Why is self-attention powerful? Because a word can look at the whole sentence
> to understand its own meaning. 'It' can look at 'animal' to resolve the pronoun.*
> *That's something an RNN can only approximate — an attention mechanism does it directly."*

**Draw on board:**

```
SELF-ATTENTION (Transformer encoder):
  Input: "The cat sat on the mat"
  Every word can look at every other word:

  "cat"  ←→ "sat"    (subject-verb)
  "it"   ←→ "cat"    (coreference)
  "mat"  ←→ "on"     (preposition)

CROSS-ATTENTION (Transformer decoder):
  Decoder state Q → looks at → Encoder outputs K, V
  (different sequences talking to each other)
```

## SECTION 1: Masked (Causal) Attention  (20 min)

> *"Here's a critical question for text generation.*
>
> *When a model is generating text — predicting the next word — it must NOT
> be allowed to look at words that come AFTER the current position.*
> *That would be cheating. You can't predict 'brown' by looking at 'fox'
> which you haven't generated yet.*
>
> *The solution: a causal mask. We mask out the upper triangle of the attention matrix.*
> *Those positions get set to negative infinity before softmax — which means
> they get zero attention weight. The decoder is blind to the future."*

**Draw on board:**

```
CAUSAL MASK (lower triangular):

          The  quick  brown  fox
The    [  1     -∞     -∞    -∞  ]
quick  [  1      1     -∞    -∞  ]
brown  [  1      1      1    -∞  ]
fox    [  1      1      1     1  ]

1 = can attend (weights computed normally)
-∞ → softmax → 0 (completely ignored)

So when generating "brown":
  - Can see: "The", "quick", "brown" (itself)
  - Cannot see: "fox" (hasn't been generated yet)
```

> *"This is how GPT works — every position can only look backwards.*
> *BERT is different — it sees everything. We'll get to that later.*
> *For now: masked attention = left-to-right generation."*

**Ask the room:**

> *"Quick sanity check — if I removed the mask during training,
> what would happen? Why would that be bad?"*

Expected answer: the model would "cheat" by looking at the answer, learn nothing useful.

> *"Exactly. The mask forces the model to actually predict.*
> *Without it, you'd train a model that has never had to generate on its own.*
> *It's like solving math problems with the answer sheet next to you."*

## SECTION 2: Bahdanau vs Vaswani — Additive vs Multiplicative  (20 min)

> *"There are two main flavors of attention invented by two different teams.*
> *Understanding them helps you read papers and understand why Transformers
> use the version they do."*

**Draw on board:**

```
ADDITIVE ATTENTION (Bahdanau 2014):
  score(q, k) = v^T × tanh(W_q × q + W_k × k)

  Think: a tiny neural network computes the score.
  Learnable weights W_q and W_k.
  More parameters, but flexible.

MULTIPLICATIVE/DOT-PRODUCT ATTENTION (Vaswani 2017):
  score(q, k) = (q · k) / sqrt(d_k)

  Think: just a dot product — like cosine similarity.
  No extra parameters.
  MUCH faster to compute (can use matrix multiplication).

Winner: Vaswani's version.
Reason: parallelizes perfectly on GPUs. Training speed dominates.
```

> *"This is a theme in ML: the method that is most parallelizable wins.*
> *Not always the most accurate. Not always the most elegant.*
> *The one that uses hardware best. Dot-product attention runs incredibly*
> *fast on GPUs because it's just matrix multiplication."*

## SECTION 4: Complexity and Scaling Considerations  (10 min)

> *"One honest limitation of attention: computational cost.*
>
> *For a sequence of length n, the attention matrix is n × n.*
> *That means attention is O(n²) in memory and computation.*
>
> *For n=512 tokens, that's 262,144 values. Manageable.*
> *For n=10,000 tokens (a long document), that's 100 million values. Problematic.*
>
> *This is why researchers built 'efficient attention' variants:*
> *Longformer, BigBird, Flash Attention — all tricks to reduce the n² cost.*
> *For now, just know: standard attention works great for sentences and short passages.*
> *Long documents need special treatment."*

**Write on board:**

```
Standard Transformer attention:  O(n²) memory
Longformer (sliding window):     O(n × window_size)
Flash Attention (recomputation): O(n²) but GPU-efficient
```

## Common Confusions and What to Say

**"I thought we already had good models with LSTMs. Why do we need this?"**
> *"LSTMs are great. For sequences up to ~100 words, they perform well.*
> *But they can't be parallelized — you must process word 5 before word 6.*
> *Attention can process all positions simultaneously on a GPU.*
> *For the scale of GPT and BERT (billions of tokens), LSTM is just too slow.*
> *Speed + scale won the game."*

**"Why is it called 'soft' attention?"**
> *"Because it's a weighted average — a soft mix of everything.*
> *Hard attention would pick ONE input and ignore all others.*
> *Soft attention takes a little from everything, proportional to relevance.*
> *Soft is used in practice because hard attention is non-differentiable —*
> *you can't backpropagate through a discrete choice."*

**"The Q/K/V thing is confusing — aren't they all just the same vectors transformed?"**
> *"Yes, great observation. In self-attention, the INPUT is the same for Q, K, V.*
> *But each gets multiplied by a DIFFERENT learned weight matrix:*
> *W_Q, W_K, W_V — each projects the same input into a different subspace.*
> *W_Q creates a 'searching' representation.*
> *W_K creates a 'being searched' representation.*
> *W_V creates a 'content' representation.*
> *These matrices are learned during training — they specialize."*

**"Why divide by sqrt(d_k) and not d_k or something else?"**
> *"It's derived from the variance of dot products.*
> *If Q and K are random vectors with unit variance,*
> *then Q·K has variance d_k.*
> *To bring that back to unit variance, divide by sqrt(d_k).*
> *Same logic as Xavier initialization — keeping activations stable."*

**"I don't see how this scales to GPT with billions of parameters."**
> *"This session is ONE attention head for ONE layer.*
> *GPT has 96 layers and 96 heads per layer.*
> *Each layer stacks on the previous, building richer representations.*
> *More layers = more abstract understanding.*
> *We'll see the full architecture in session 4."*
